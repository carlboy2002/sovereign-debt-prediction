Beyond Official Statistics: Predicting Sovereign Debt Crises in Emerging Markets Using Satellite Imagery and News Text
An alternative-data early warning framework combining nighttime lights, global news sentiment, and macroeconomic indicators.
Jinen Wang  ·  Levi Liu  ·  Wuhao Xia  ·  Chenyi Jiang
Applying Machine Learning — Final Project Dashboard
N = 80 emerging market countries T = 2000–2023 k = 76 features Instructor: Prof. Daniel Björkegren
Abstract

Sovereign debt crises in emerging markets consistently blindside international institutions that rely on official macroeconomic statistics — data that are released with lags and frequently manipulated by governments whose solvency is in question. We propose an alternative-data early warning framework combining NASA nighttime satellite imagery (as a proxy for true economic activity), GDELT global news sentiment, and standard World Bank macroeconomic indicators.

Across a panel of approximately 80 emerging market countries from 2000 to 2023, we train nested XGBoost classifiers and an L₁-regularized baseline, grounded in a two-period structural signaling game. The full model achieves validation AUC of 0.902; adding news text to the macro baseline lifts average precision by 14.8%. A novel "misreporting index" — the gap between satellite and official growth — matches the predictions of our structural model with a simulated-empirical correlation match of −0.037 vs. −0.035.

A fairness audit reveals meaningful regional heterogeneity: performance is strongest in Latin America (AUC 0.944) and weakest in East Asia & Pacific (0.803), reflecting the concentration of training signal in regions with historical crisis precedents. We close with a transparent discussion of data-quality limitations and directions for future work.

Sovereign debt crises are difficult to anticipate not because the relevant information is absent — but because much of it is deliberately withheld from the statistical record.

Traditional early warning systems rely on official statistics that arrive late and can be manipulated by the very governments whose solvency is in question. We test whether two alternative, manipulation-resistant data streams — satellite-derived nighttime lights and global news sentiment — can detect distress signals that official data misses. The answer appears to be yes, but with important caveats about who benefits from such systems and who does not.

Across 80+ emerging market countries and 24 years of data, we construct a structural prediction framework grounded in a two-period signaling game. Governments facing debt distress choose whether to misreport; the gap between satellite-derived activity and official GDP growth — what we term the divergence index — should carry predictive information about subsequent crisis onset.

Interpretation

LASSO validation AUC is 0.666, well below XGBoost's 0.902. This gap is itself informative: it suggests the predictive power of our features lies in their nonlinear interactions, not in any single linear effect. The top LASSO features — declining reserves, widening current account deficits, negative news tone — nonetheless tell a coherent economic story.

IV · Structural Signaling Model

A two-period game predicts when governments misreport.

Before fitting any predictive model, we develop a structural framework: the government decides whether to misreport true growth (period 1), then either services debt or defaults (period 2).

Policy implication

If this model were deployed to allocate IMF surveillance attention, countries in East Asia & Pacific and high-income economies — where crises are rarer and signals more idiosyncratic — would likely receive systematically under-calibrated warnings. Any operational deployment should include region-specific threshold calibration.

Caveat

The misreporting index is exploratory. Several data-quality issues limit its reliability: the mismatch between DMSP (2000–2013) and VIIRS (2014–present) satellite sensors; VIIRS algorithm revisions around 2016; and saturation effects in developed economies. A production-ready version would require sensor harmonization following Li et al. (2020). We present these rankings as a methodological illustration, not a final product.

VII · Limitations and Future Work

Where our current pipeline falls short.

Transparent acknowledgment of data and methodological constraints; a roadmap for what a production system would require.

Data quality

The DMSP–VIIRS sensor transition in 2013–2014 produces artifactual jumps in the nightlight time series that overwhelm the subtle signals we aim to detect. Our current workaround (VIIRS-only, 2014–present) reduces the effective sample by 40%.

GDELT coverage of small low-income countries is sparse; this biases text features toward larger, more-reported-on economies.

Methodology

LASSO convergence issues at our chosen regularization strength suggest the feature space is poorly conditioned. A future iteration should explore PCA preprocessing or elastic-net regularization.

Our original causal identification strategy — VIIRS expansion as a natural experiment — proved infeasible once actual coverage was inspected. A more credible DiD would use commodity price shocks as exogenous variation.

The divergence between what governments report and what satellites observe should, in principle, carry predictive information. Extracting it cleanly remains a harder problem than our pipeline currently solves.
